General

THE SETTLERS III Editor enables you to create your own new single and multi-player maps for THE SETTLERS III. It doesn't allow you to alter maps that have already been designed, such as the maps for the original campaigns or the single or multi-player maps created by Blue Byte.

Copyright Notice: Please take notice that the SETTLERS III Level Editor is an extension to your personal copy of the SETTLERS III game and as such is intended exclusively for private use. Any commercial use of THE SETTLERS III Level Editor and any maps created while using it requires written consent from Blue Byte Software, Inc.

 

Map Formats

THE SETTLERS III Level Editor offers you two formats for saving maps that you have created or are in the process of creating. The first format has the extension .edm. This format is the one you want to use as long as you are still in the process of designing your map or if you wish to save a partially finished map as a template map for future variations.

Say, for example, that you want to create a series of maps based on the concept of "The Settlers go to Florida". The Level Editor allows you to create an island into the shape of the of Florida, lavish it with appropriately beautiful beaches and palm trees, and even create an otherwise non-existent mountain range if you like. A little imagination, several variations on the initial starting locations or the amounts of resources available for your settlers can really enhance the replayability of THE SETTLERS III. By changing the weighting of magic or military strengths, you could effectively create an experience that even THE SETTLERS III designers never thought of.

The second format that your SETTLERS III Level Editor offers you has the extension .map. This is the format you want to use when you are satisfied with the map you have created and wish to save it so that no further changes can be made. When you save your map in this format, the map can no longer be opened with the map editor and thus can no longer be changed.

After you have created your map, you'll of course want to play it and offer others the same possibility. Here again, it is very important to keep in mind the differences between the two formats. The .edm format should only be given to others when you want to work cooperatively on the creation of a new map or a series of maps. For multiplayer play, it is essential that the .map format is used and that the maps are identical. If players in a multiplayer game use maps with the same name which are not identical, this will lead to quirky behavior in the game and to system crashes!

.edm for designing and editing
.map for playing

 

Placing Terrain, Objects and Raw Materials

The term terrain is meant to include all forms of land and water, as well as elevation for hills and mountains, and depths for seas and lakes. All forms of the terrain, including grass, desert, swamps, and rivers can be found in the Tool Window under "Set Type of Terrain". You will also find the tools for elevating the land or setting depth for water here as well.

The term Object is meant to include trees, stone, graves, buildings, goods piles, and even Settlers.

The term Raw Materials is meant to include mining products such as gold and iron, as well as fish.

All forms of terrain, objects and raw materials are placed on the map via the Design Window using your mouse. Please note - where you place things on your map is subject to certain restrictions. For example, you may only place mineral resources under rocky terrain. To do this, click in the "Tool Window" on the terrain, object, or type of material you want to use, choose from the "Tool Setup Window" the type of brush you want to use, and place it or paint it in your map in the Design Area.

It is strongly recommended that you proceed in the order described above. First, create your terrain and set its elevation; then place your objects on the terrain, and finally place the raw materials in your map. In the section, "A Closer Look at the Tool Window", you'll find detailed information about restrictions on placing the different types of objects and raw materials on various kinds of terrain.

To replace one form of terrain with another, you simply "paint" over the existing terrain. Note - if you "paint" water on terrain that you've elevated (hilly or mountainous terrain), the elevation will be dropped back to zero or sea level.

Under certain circumstances the Level Editor won't allow you to place certain buildings or settlers where you want. For example, you cannot place raw materials in a desert, or fish in swamps. You'll find more information about this in the sections "Setting Buildings" and "Setting Settlers". When a building or settler cannot be placed in a certain terrain, it will be appear grayed-out. When an object or raw material is placed over terrain where it is allowable, it will be displayed in its normal color.

 

Deleting Terrain, Objects and Raw Materials

Whereas you can place objects on the map by simply clicking with the map, when you delete objects, you have to specify which object you want to delete beforehand.

For this you have the following buttons or items:

In the Tool Window you'll find an item in every subdirectory that contains objects designated "Delete <Object name>". When this item is selected, you can delete the corresponding objects by simply clicking on them. It is also possible to select an object from this group and then while holding the "Ctrl" key depressed, click on the object on the map.The object will then be deleted.

When you select "Delete <Object name>" and at the same time hold the "Shift" key down, all objects and settlers under your mouse pointer will be deleted. This doesn't apply to buildings and raw materials.

The same thing happens when you activate the item "Delete All" in the Tool Window.

In general, there are objects which can be placed and deleted with brushes of various sizes. A forest, for example, can be placed on the map or deleted with a bold brush.. But, on the other hand, there are objects which are not so well suited for mass placement or mass deletion. There is no need for painting 20 woodcutter's huts onto the map at one stroke. Checking your Map in the Game

So that you can view how your map looks under playing conditions, and decide whether you're satisfied with it or not, the game allows you to load a temporary version of your map into the game. Note – in this mode the fog of war is not activated and you'll be able to see the entire map; you cannot play in this mode, though.

By pressing the "Esc" key, you can leave this modus and return to the Level Editor.

 

Playing Self-made Maps

After you've finished editing a map and have exported it, the map will be saved automatically in the subdirectory "User" in the directory "Map" of your SETTLERS3 installation directory.

If you've downloaded a map from the Internet or have received a map from a friend, you should save this map in the same subdirectory – the "User" folder. The maps function when they are saved in other directories but in the case of multiplayer games, the map must be saved in the same folder by all players who participate in such a game.

Every player participating in a multiplayer game must also have the corresponding map at his disposal and this map must also be the identical version for all of the players. Naturally, no one can play a map where the Amazons are present as a race or where Amazon goods such as honey pots have been put on the map unless they have the Amazon CD. These maps will automatically be identified as Amazon maps and displayed only to players who have the Amazon CD.

Please don't forget: Everybody who wants to play must have this map in the same folder as the the player who has initiated the game! Get in touch with one another (via Chat, for example) and make sure that the player who has initiated the game hasn't moved the map to the "Multi" folder!
