The Computer Opponent

It is pointless to search for reasons why artificial intelligence today and in the foreseeable future is and will not be a match for human creativity and inspiration—despite some dazzling advances in processor speed. As map designer, you'll want to give the computer player some initial advantages over its human opponent so that the map is a challenge. Naturally, you can exert your influence in determining where your computer opponent is especially strong and where he needs more time to develop.

The artificial intelligence in THE SETTLERS III doesn't dance to the traditional music: You won't discover that, after 24 minutes, the computer attacks with 80% of its soldiers, provided that the human player has built 10 towers and hasn't yet drunk more than three cups of coffee. The artificial intelligence in THE SETTLERS III tries to behave like a human player does: based on the situation. And sometimes, like its human counterpart, it makes mistakes. But the advantage then, is that not every game proceeds identically.

 

General Stuff

Provide your computer opponent with enough room and enough raw materials nearby. Planning in advance isn't one of the strengths of a computer opponent. An available construction site will be used for a building even if the location could be better used later for another building. Most often, the computer opponent will simply build where it finds space.

The computer opponent is also not able to expand in a planned way. It simply checks, for example, where there is space on the border to build a guard tower. If you put important resources far outside his borders, it will take a long time before he can exploit them.

When in doubt, give your computer opponent a tool, a settler, or a pile of goods too many than one too few.

Making your computer opponent fit for human competition requires that he be a competent architect of his settlement, that he have adequate fighting strength, and that he have the ability to make use of priests for engaging his human counterparts.

 

The Computer as a Settlement Planner

If you supply your computer opponent from the very beginning with two or three woodcutters, a forester, two or three stonecutters, plenty of carriers, shovels and hammers, and give him enough space to build, he will expand quickly, and rapidly develop a fully functional infrastructure.

The Computer as a Fighter

If you want to create a computer opponent that is militarily strong from the beginning of the game, you have to avoid making a basic mistake.

Never give your computer opponent more than double as many "free" soldiers (soldiers, who aren't garrisoned in guard towers) as his human counterpart.

There is a simple reason for this. In this situation, the computer opponent will immediately attempt to overrun his enemies immediately after the game begins.

Try instead to create conditions where the computer opponent can get off to a solid start by producing the additional soldiers he'll need. By doing this, the human player will be put under pressure to increase his military strength.

Final Considerations

  • The artificial intelligence makes use of random events. For this reason, the computer player won't always develop identically in every game. This can lead to the map being winnable in one game, but not in another.
    Make sure that your computer opponent can be beat in at least 90% of all cases.
  • The computer player isn't able to use ships. Therefore, he cannot attack human players who occupy an island alone. Keep this in mind when you set your victory conditions. A human player who settles an island alone can never be defeated by the computer player.
  • All of the values given in the tables you've seen above are by no means chiseled in stone, but rather offer basic orientation for getting started. After a while you'll develop a feeling for what your computer opponent needs for the special situations that your maps present. But, in case something doesn't work out, these tables will allow you to check elements you might have forgotten along the way. Sometimes the difference between a playable and unplayable map could rest on a single tool.
