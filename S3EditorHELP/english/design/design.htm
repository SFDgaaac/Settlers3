Designing a SETTLERS III Map

Creating a New Map and Setting its Size

After you've started your SETTLERS III Level Editor, arrange the three windows so that they are comfortable for you to work with. Then you can open the file menu and click on the item, New. A window will appear where you'll be asked to decide on the size of the map that you wish to create. The map that we'll be going through together in this section is 448 x 448. Please select this size now.

Before creating a map, it is advisable to have a good idea how it is supposed to look when you've finished because you can only set the size of your map at the very beginning. Changing the size of your map later is not possible.

Selecting the largest format 768 x 768 for all your maps is also not a wise alternative because larger formats require more computer time and all your maps would require more time to play through. The best alternative is a little forethought and maybe a couple sketches.

Please open now your "Edit" menu and select "Edit Map Settings...". Another window will appear in which you can select the setting "Single Player". Please do so. As you may have noticed, we want to create a map in which a single player plays against the computer.

We will ignore the setting "Stock Default" because we want to set these values ourselves by hand.

 

Deciding on the Form of your Island World

When you started your Level Editor, the "Tool Window" and the "Tool Setup" Window were opened as well as the Design Window. If one of these three windows is missing, go to the "View" menu and click on the menu item corresponding to the window which is not open.

In your Tool Window, expand the "Set Type of Terrain" directory by clicking on the "+" sign to the left of the entry. A list of items will appear in the familiar form of subdirectories. Choose the item "Grass", please.

Now, move over to your "Tool Setup" Window to choose the "bold" brush. Make sure the options "Spray" and "Sprinkle" are not active. If they are active, please deactivate them. We can ignore the adjustments under "Player" for the time being. This will be covered soon.

Now you are ready to "paint" an island in your work area. Hold you left mouse button depressed as you glide across the work area. So that we can work through this together, paint your island so that it looks approximately similar to the one shown below.

 

Defining the Start Postions of the Players

Please look over to your icon bar above the design area now and click on the "x2" zoom icon. Hold down your right mouse button over the Design Area, you'll notice that you can scroll your map. Do this now so that you have the southern half of your island as close to the middle of the window as possible.

Return to the "Tool Setup" Window and click on the button "Edit Players". A new window will immediately appear with the title "Player Settings". 

Take a look at your "Player Settings" to be sure that the active player has the number 1. If not, please change it to 1 now. Now click on the button "Use current screen position". This player's race should be the Romans.

In the "Team Data" block, choose "Human" for "Played by:" and set the team number at 0.

The settings for Player 1 should now be:

Race: Romans
Start X: 260 (approx.)
Start Y: 400 (approx.)
Played by: Human
Team: 0

Now, please click on the button "Add Player". You'll notice that the value for the active player has risen to 2. Now please repeat the process for player 2 exactly as you did for the first player. This time, though, you want to scroll your design area so that the northern half of the island is centered in your Design Window. This time, choose the Egyptians as race, and enter "Computer" after "Played by:" So that the Level Editor knows that Player 1 and Player 2 are not allies but rather opponents, assign the second player (computer) the team number 1.

The settings for Player 2 should now be:

Race: Egyptians
Start X: 220 (approx.)
Start Y: 60 (approx.)
Played by: Computer
Team: 1

Do not skip numbers when you assign team numbers because this will cause the Level Editor problems.

 

 

Improving the Looks of your Coastline

Close the "Player Settings" window, please. If you look at it in game view, you'll see that the coastline looks similar to the picture above. To take a look at your map in game view, go to the "File" menu and click on "View Map In Game".

The coastline doesn't look so impressive but it could look like the coastline shown below.

After you've looked at your map, use the "Esc" key to return to the Level Editor so that you can continue working on your map.

In order to create such a natural looking coastline, you only need the right settings for your tools and a little patience.

To begin with, change to zoom level 4 focusing in on a piece of your coastline. Go to the "Tool Window" and choose "Grass" once again, this time choosing "Thin" as your brush size. In addition, activate the "Sprinkle" function. Glide along the coastline being careful to avoid long, straight edges. With a little practice, you'll soon develop a feeling for doing this. This same procedure can be used not only for borders between water and shore, but also for the borders between the shoreline and grassland.

You can repeat this process using "Point" instead of "Thin" to roughen up the last straight stretches.

As the final step, please change the selected terrain from grass to shore. Leave your brush size at "Point" and move along the perimeter of your entire island at zoom level 4 so that you can be sure of not missing any straight edges.

Check your results in the game once more, in the same way you did before. Remember to press the "Esc" key to return to the Level Editor.

If any places are not yet up to expectations, continue improving them until you're satisfied with the results. There is another possibility of roughening up the borders. Instead of using grass as your point of departure, you can also choose water. Open the subdirectory for "Water", clicking on the "+" sign and then choose "Water Level 1".

Choose "Thin" as your brush size with "Sprinkle" activated, just as you did with "Grass". Of course, you can effectively use these settings for all types of terrain when you want to roughen up the border.

Delete small sandbars in the water because the Level Editor will view these as separate islands. The small sandbars could also complicate the unloading of soldiers and goods.

 

Roughening the Landscape

Just as straight coastlines do not appeal to the eyes, completely flat land has very little visual interest.

In order to introduce some rolling landscape into your map, go to the "Edit" menu and choose "Roughen Landscape...".

In the new window that appears, adjust the settings as follows:

Altitude Differences: Medium
Options: Brush variation: On
Number of Peaks: Low
Brush Size: Bold

 

Save your map before you roughen the landscape! This function involves extensive calculation for the Level Editor. For this reason, it may take a while before you can see the effect. Take a look at your map in game view to see if whether the landscape doesn't look much better now. It should be similar to the landscape shown below.

 

If you are satisfied, we can go on to the next step. If not, open your previous map again. Because of the extensive calculation required for the "Roughen Landscape..." function, "Redo" is not available. (This was the reason for saving the map under a different name, a kind of back up so to speak).

 

Raising Mountains

Now it is time to start giving thought to what makes a map exciting and what you have to watch out for while designing your landscape or terrain. At the end of the following sections, you'll find a block of text dealing with questions of strategy, which arise in conjunction with the material, techniques and procedures described in each section.

But next we are going to place two mountains on our map. Go to the Tool Window and choose "Raise Terrain (Medium)". You'll find this under "Height Tools". Choose the following settings from the "Tool Setup" Window, please.

Brush: Customized: Custom Size: 25 (Use the arrow buttons to increase or decrease the value)
Sprinkle: On
Spray: Off

The "Selected Player" number is not important here because mountains don't belong to any player, even though they might control them.

Switch to zoom level 1 ("x1" on the icon bar). Paint two elongated mountains chains on the terrain on the large landmass in the middle of the island, one in the east and one in the west. Make sure that "Sprinkle" is still activated. This may be a bit slower but leads to better looking results.

Begin "lifting" the terrain until you have two banana-shaped forms on both sides of the landmass in the middle of your map. The lighter the green becomes the higher your mountains. Make sure that your mountains vary in height going over some area more often than others.

Now return to the "Tool Setup" Window and change your brush to "Medium" and deactivate "Sprinkle". You are now ready to change the terrain of your mountains into rocky terrain. Clean up the borders between the rocky terrain and the grassland with a Thin Grass Sprinkle.

To top everything off, put a little bit of snow on the highest peaks in your mountain range. Save your map and then take a look at it in game view. Do the mountains look the way you imagined them? If they are not quite up to par, go back and make improvements where necessary. Here again, you'll develop a feeling for this quickly.

So that the various parties don't have so far to go to get to the mineral resources, we can create a couple small hills. Put one hill in the west on the southern landmass for the Romans and the other hill in the east on the northern landmass for the Egyptians.

Your map should look approximately as shown below.

STRATEGY CORNER
You can use mountains for concealing mineral resources: coal, iron ore, gold, gems and sulfur. The decision about where you hide them is, of course, of major strategic significance. This begins with the location of the mountains. Naturally, it's pleasant for a player to be able to find everything he needs on his front door step, but not exactly exciting. The player, especially one beyond the novice stage, should first have to send out his thieves to find out where the mountains are.

After sending his spies out, he knows in what direction he must expand. In our case, the nearest mountains for the Romans are in the west and northwest and for the Egyptians in the south and south-southeast.

Because the small hills can only contain small amounts of raw materials, the players are forced to expand toward one another in order to get to the resources they'll need on the main island.

 

Buildings

Before beginning to place raw materials, trees, stone, goods and other objects on your map, you should give some thought to where the players will need them. Especially when one or more of the players is computer controlled, a moderately well developed settlement should be in place so that the computer player isn't strongly disadvantaged.

Go to your "Tool Window" and choose from the categories of buildings under "Set Buildings" the buildings you want to place on your map. For buildings, the brush should be set for "Point" and the "Sprinkle" and "Spray" functions should be deactivated. It is, however, very important that the correct player is activated, because, otherwise, the Level Editor won't allow you to place the building on your map.

We will choose the following buildings for getting started on this map.

STRATEGY CORNER
  • Place the buildings in the Egyptian colony so that two woodcutter's huts are located in the west with a forester’s hut between them.
  • Place a sawmill a little to the east. To the north of the sawmill, the Egyptians have a stretch of coast, where you can place a fisherman's hut.
  • To the east of the fisherman's hut you can place two stonecutter's huts with some space between them.
  • Since the Egyptians need a lot of stone for building, a computer opponent playing the Egyptians should have no fewer than two stonecutter's huts at the beginning.
  • You should also place a toolmaker's works and a weapon maker's works as well as an iron smelting works near the hill.
  • The human player, with the advantage of creative intelligence, doesn't need nearly as much to get off to a good start.
  • You can place a woodcutter's hut and a sawmill west of the tower and a stonecutter's hut in the east.

Important buildings such as residences, mines, barracks, etc. should be placed in the control zone of several guard towers and not too near the opponent or they will be very vulnerable to attack. Protect your important buildings now with additional guard towers without increasing Egyptian territory more than necessary.

Computer controlled players should NEVER have physically divided settlements. Even a single settlement area divided into two halves by a sea or snow so that the civilian settlers have no opportunity to go from one side to the other will lead to problems that cannot be resolved by artificial intelligence. The development of your computer player won't be satisfactory.

STRATEGY CORNER
You should pay attention to a couple of things when you place settlers on your map:

Civil occupations:
For every manual worker's building, you should also place the appropriate craftsman on your map. You should also not forget carriers, diggers, and builders. Without them, nothing functions. Apart from the number of craftsmen you place on your map, which depends on the buildings you've put on your map, you should aim for a balance of strength between the players, weighing in the inherent weaknesses of computer players.

Military occupations:
The matter of balanced strength applies especially in this case. When one player has distinct advantages in strength, you can expect that this player will choose to attack early in the game.

Forests and Fields of Stone

Our next step involves placing forests and fields of stones on the map so that an initial supply of building materials is available to the settlers at the beginning of the game. Those types of trees and stone that only serve a decorative function will be dealt with below.

A bit of forest should now be placed somewhere near the woodcutter's huts of the two players. This forest doesn't have to be enormous. A forester can renew the supply of trees as the game progresses. Go to the "Tool Window" and choose "Set Forest". You'll find this under "Set Objects/Auto Generation". Then choose a "Thin" brush from the "Tool Setup" Window and activate the "Sprinkle" function. The "Spray" function should be set to off. The setting for the "Player" that is currently active is not important when you place forests and fields of stone on your map.

You can use the same procedure for setting the fields of stone in your map, merely changing the selected tool from "Set Forest" to "Set Stone"

When you've finished, your settlement should look similar to the map shown below:

The red ellipses show the forest areas.
The blue ellipses show the areas with larger quantities of building stone.

STRATEGY CORNER
  • A large forest would be a disadvantage for Egyptian players because their need for wood as a building material is relatively modest and the excess forest would act as an obstacle to expansion.
  • Asian players, in contrast, needs a larger forest to fill their building needs. (And more woodcutters, foresters and sawmill workers as well)
  • It is important that your settlers repeatedly find new supplies of building materials as they expand their colonies. This is especially important for fields of building stone which, unlike forests, cannot be renewed during the game.
  • Fields of stone can also be placed on your map as obstacles. Clearing them for building activities is time-consuming (this is also true for thick forests). In addition, both forests and fields of stone can effectively block the movement of instruments of war.
  • Keep in mind that fields of stone that are made up of stones not suitable for building can't be cleared and function as permanent barriers.

 

Rivers

Generally, rivers are placed on a map relatively late in the process. Rivers have a very central significance. Without rivers, there is no water; without water no food production; except fish; without food, no coal, gold, or iron, no sulfur and no gems.

To place a river on the map, go to the "Tool Window" and open the subdirectory for "Rivers". You'll find this under "Set Type of Terrain". First, we can set the size for the river. We can set the brush at "Point" and make sure that the "Sprinkle" and "Spray" functions are deactivated. The "Player" settings are of no importance for placing rivers on the map.

Please paint three rivers onto your map using the picture below as a guide. The rivers are located within the blue ellipses:

When you place rivers on your maps, keep in mind that rivers flow from higher elevations to lower ones. The higher elevations on your map will be displayed in progressively lighter tones and the lower elevations in darker tones. Here you'll be able to see if your rivers are flowing upward anywhere along their course. You can then work on the landscape with your "Height Tools" to eliminate any unnatural behavior of your rivers. The Level Editor won't prevent you from placing a river on the map which flows up one side of the mountain and down the other, but such a river will certainly cause some eyebrows to rise.

Rivers don't have constant widths. Vary the width of your rivers, remembering that rivers tend to become wider as they get closer to the sea. When you are working on your rivers, adjust their widths only from the "Tool Window". Do not use a larger brush size than "Point". In the Design Area, your rivers will always appear to be one pixel wide, even when you are using "River": Width 4. When you take a look at your river in game view, you will see the variations in width that you have used. Make sure that there are no breaks in your rivers, though. Especially on slower computers, there could be gaps in your river, if you move your mouse too quickly when painting the river onto the map. If there are any gaps in your river, go back and fill them in.

STRATEGY CORNER
It is very important that each of the players has at least one stretch of river nearby. It should also be possible to build two waterworks along this stretch of river because in many cases one is not enough.

Rivers don't act as obstacles for the settlers. Keep in mind, though, that river deltas take up a fair amount of space that could otherwise be used for building.

 

Resources

Fish

Fish can only be placed in seas and lakes, not in rivers. Placing fish far from the shore makes no sense because the fisherman cannot get to them. Where you put the fish and how many you want to have is up to you. Please remember that the Level Editor has to keep track of all the fish though and that takes time. Moreover, when a player has an almost unlimited number of fish, there is far less incentive to build up other forms of food production. Three or four fishermen can catch enough fish to keep a larger number of mines running.

Go to the "Tool Window" and choose "Set Fish (Water)". You'll find this under "Set Resources / Set Resource Type". Go to the "Tool Setup" Window now and choose a "Thin" brush and activate the "Sprinkle" function and the "Spray" function with "Spray density" set at 25. When you place resources on the map, the number of the "Selected Player" in not important.

Since we've already built a fisherman's hut for the Egyptians, let's put some fish nearby to keep the fisherman busy. If you'd like to place more fish around your map now, feel free to do so.

STRATEGY CORNER
Since fish represent a source of food that can be obtained early, they can be used effectively for adjusting difficulty levels on the map. Without any fish whatsoever, all of the players will have a considerably more difficult time developing.

 

Mineral Resources

You can place mineral resources on the map in much the same way as you've just done for fish. Mineral resources can only be placed in areas where you have rocky terrain, usually near mountains. Before you begin, please make sure that the "Sprinkle" and "Spray" functions are deactivated. In the picture below you can see where to put the mineral resources.

The colors show where mineral deposits are located.

Coal = Black
Iron Ore = Orange
Gold = Yellow
Gems = Red

 

STRATEGY CORNER
  • Don't make it too easy for the players on your map, but not impossible either. A player without iron or coal cannot produce weapons for his soldiers and will also not be able to expand very easily.
  • In this map the nearest coal deposits for the Romans are located pretty far away. Here the player leading the Romans should be informed that he should make use of his charcoal maker to convert wood into coal until he can get to the nearest deposits of coal.
  • The nearest deposits of gold and gems are also relatively far away from both players. The Roman player should be informed as well that it is in his best interests to prevent the Egyptians from reaching the gem deposits in the southeastern region of the main landmass in the middle of the map.
  • In order for the Roman player to be able to make use of the gold deposits in the northwest of this land mass, he will have to expand his territory quite a bit.
  • The placement of the resources makes it likely that a long border between the two players will form. This usually makes for a very interesting game.

 

The Final, Strategically Important Changes

Having reached this point in the design of the map, you can well imagine that it could become even more interesting by building in a couple more challenges for the player. In the map we can see that the gem deposits in the northeast of the main land mass is easy for the Romans to reach. The Romans shouldn't have any real difficulties in depriving the Asians of access to these important deposits. As soon as the Romans take over this area, the Egyptian player (Computer) will have little chance of getting to the gem deposits.

There is a simple possibility for changing the area so that it's more difficult for the Romans to settle, but gives the Egyptians a fighting chance: a desert.

Go to the "Tool Window" and choose "Desert". This item is under "Set Type of Terrain". From the "Tool Setup" Window, please adjust your brush to "Medium" and make sure that that the "Sprinkle" function and "Spray" function are deactivated. It doesn't matter which "Player" is currently active.

Paint a desert onto your map now using the picture below as a guide.

If there are any objects on the land that you want to convert to desert, you'll have to delete them, otherwise the Level Editor will not carry the conversion out or will paint around the objects.

Still more challenges are, of course, possible: stone fields, swamps, thick forests and lakes, just to mention a few. Continue working with this map, trying out different ideas that occur to you until you are satisfied with the results.

 

Sprucing up the Colony

When you look through the objects available under "Set Objects" in the "Tool Window", you'll quickly notice that there are long lists of objects in several categories. When the time comes for sprucing up the landscape, you'll find everything from toadstools to cactus plants and from dead trees to skeletons of dead animals. Give them all a try adding as much detail to your landscape as you want.

Not all objects can be placed on all types of terrain. Many of the restrictions are what you might expect—you can't put a shipwreck in the mountains, for example, unless you find a way to get the Level Editor to make rain for 40 days and 40 nights. An object will be displayed in tones of gray when it cannot be placed in a particular terrain and in color where it is possible.

You should try playing your maps several times. The artificial intelligence in the game doesn't behave identically for all games. You might find that during the first game that the map is very difficult but playable. That could be an exception where the artificial intelligence was dozing and that the map is actually in most cases unplayable.

Take a good look at your map, try out different possibilities and, above all, make sure that a computer opponent has a reasonable opportunity to expand. As a rule, a computer player will need more room that a creative, human player.
