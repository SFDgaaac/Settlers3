The Level Editor - The Menus

Starting the Level Editor

In order to start THE SETTLERS III Level Editor, go to your "Start" menu and click on the item "Blue Byte/SETTLERS3 Level Editor/SETTLERS III Level Editor".

We recommend that you set the color palette of your monitor to High Color (16 bit) to obtain optimal performance from the Level Editor.

 

Work Area

After starting the Editor, the first thing you'll see is your Work Area. It consists of a Design Window with a menu bar, a icon bar, and information bar as well as two tool windows. You are free to arrange the windows as you see fit.

 

The Menu Bar

All of the functions you need for working on a SETTLERS III map can be found in the menu bar. These include creating, loading and saving maps, as well as a wide variety of editing and viewing functions. You'll find more detailed information below in the section "A Closer Look at the Menu Bar"

 

The Icon Bar

The Icon Bar consists of seventeen buttons, all of which include a tool tip. From left to right, they are ”Create new map, Open map, Save current map, Undo, Redo, Zoom Levels 1, 1.5, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and   8, Show Objects, Show Buildings, Show Settlers, and Show Piles of Goods.”

 

The Design Area

The design area is the large black area below your menu bar where you have an overview of the map you are creating. Right after starting the Map Editor, you'll find a default world in the shape of a parallelogram sized 256 x 256. This new world is initially only a dark blue void with a red thumb tack in the middle.

A 256 x 256 map is naturally not very large. If you want to create a larger world, you'll have to make a larger map. How you do this will be explained in the section "A Closer Look at the Menu Bar". Whenever you create a new map, you'll be asked at the beginning how large the map is supposed to be. After you have made your choice and have begun designing your world, a size change is no longer possible.

In order to navigate in the design area, just hold your right mouse button down and move your mouse in the direction you want to go.

 

The Information Bar

The information bar supplies you with details concerning the size of your map, the current position of your mouse pointer and the version number of your Map Editor.

 

The Tool Windows

In addition to the design area, you'll also find two tool windows which you'll often use while creating your SETTLERS world. The upper window is your "Tool Setup" Window and the one below is your "Tool Window". You'll find that you use both these windows interactively while you are shaping your world. More detailed information about these windows will be offered in the section "A Closer Look at the Tool Stetup Windows".

 

A Closer Look at the Menu Bar

In the Menu Bar you'll find four pull down menu items: File, Edit, View, and Help. The fly down menus can be opened in the normal way with a left mouse click or with the keyboard with ALT + the underlined letter.

 

The "File" Menu

The File menu contains eight items: New..., Load..., Save, Save As..., Export Game Map File, Start Game With Map and Quit. The three dots after some items indicate that a new window will open offering you further functions appropriate to that menu item.

New...

To create a new map, click on the menu item "File" and then "New". You can also simply click on the first icon from the left in the menu bar.

A list window will appear where you can choose the size of your SETTLERS III world. Choose one of the sizes available in the list box. The bigger the world you select, the more people can comfortably play but also the slower the performance of your PC. Larger maps are not recommended for play on a P100, for instance.

Once you've chosen the size of your world, it can no longer be changed. Before you make your choice, think about how big your world will have to be to get your ideas into it.

 

Open...

To load a map that you've already begun designing, click on the menu item "File" and then "Open". You can also click on the second icon from the left in the menu bar.

You can only open files in the Level Editor which have been saved with the extension .edm. Files which have been saved with the extension .map cannot be opened for further editing. This ensures that you can decide whether others can contribute to the creation of your world or that the world remains as you intended it to be.

A "Load File" window will appear where you can choose which map you wish to open for further designing. Simply click on the file you wish to load into the Map Editor and then click on the "OK" button. You can also simply double click on the file in the window to load it.

 

Save

The first time you save a new map it doesn't matter whether you click on "Save..." or "Save As..." In both cases a window will appear in which you'll be asked where and under what name you want to save your map.

BE CAREFUL: If you have made changes in an existing map and then click on the "Save...", your current map will be saved in the same place with the same name. Your original map with the same name will be overwritten.

The first time you save a file, you only decide where and under what name you want to save an *.edm file. All *.map files will automatically be saved in the "User" subdirectory of the "Map" directory under Settlers3.

 

Save As...

If you want to save a newly created map or want to save a loaded map under a new name, this is the best way to do so. Just as the case when you first save a file, you can only decide where and under what name you want to save an *.edm file. All *.map files will be saved automatically in your "Map" directory in the "User" subdirectory.

 

Export Game Map File

In order to be able to play the map that you've designed, you have to export it. The map will be exported, automatically checked for errors, and saved in the *.map format.

 

View Map in Game

When you click on this menu item, you can take a look at the most recent version of your current map in the game. The fog of war is cleared in this case so that you can see the entire map. You cannot, however, play the map using this function!

As long as THE SETTLERS III is running, you cannot activate your map in the Level Editor. A text window will appear letting you know that the game is still open.

This function only works in combination with the newest version of THE SETTLERS III. If this is not the case, this menu point will appear gray, indicating that it is deactivated. The newest version is currently the version on the SETTLERS III Mission CD. If you decide to uninstall THE SETTLERS III but continue using the Level Editor, you should make a note to update your version of THE SETTLERS III to the version number of the Editor when you reinstall SETTLERS III.

 

Save  As Bitmap

This menu item allows you to create a screenshot of the current map as a picture in bitmap format (.bmp). The entire map will be saved, not only the part that you can view in the design area. The map will be saved with the current viewing settings. This includes

  • Zoom level
  • Resouce view
  • Island view
  • Objects displayed
  • all other features, which can be set in the "View" menu.

Since the bitmaps can be really large, the following precautions have been built in:

  • Bitmaps up to 48 MBytes will be created without any warning message.
  • Bitmaps between 49 MBytes and 1 GByte will display a warning message, allowing you to cancel the process.
  • Bitmaps over 1 GByte will not be exported.

The Level Editor will display a progress bar while the picture is being rendered. The rendering process can be stopped at any time. Whenever the rendering process is stopped, the bitmap file will be deleted. In addition, the following checks have been built in.

  • Rendering will be stopped and the target file deleted if an error occurs while writing.
  • Write-protected files will not be allowed as target files.
  • Be careful!  Existing files with the same name will be overwritten without a warning message.
  • The Level Editor will check the amount of free space on the target drive. If there isn't at least one and a half times as much free space as needed, an error message will be displayed. If the target drive is a net drive, the Level Editor will not always be able to determine the amount of free space. This will depend of the version of the operating system. (Win 95, for example, has a mapping of the net drive as a precondition). In this case the check will not be carried out.
  • Note: During the rendering process, ALL functions in all windows will be deactivated. The only functioning window will be the dialog window with the progress indicator.

 

Quit

When you have finished working on your SETTLERS world, you can click here to close the Editor. If you haven't yet saved your current map, the program will ask you to do so.

 

The "Edit" Menu

The Edit menu contains nine items: Undo and Redo, Edit Map Settings, Edit Player Settings and Victory Conditions, Set All Resources to Default and Remove All Resources, Roughen Landscape, and Check Map.

Undo

If you make a mistake while editing your map, you can cancel the last action you have taken by clicking on this item.

For certain actions, which require a large amount of memory, you can't use the undo function. This includes actions such as "Roughen Landscape", deleting foundation buildings such as guard towers and castles and deleting all resources using the "Remove All Resources" function under "Edit".

 

Redo

If after using the undo function, you realize that you haven't made a mistake after all, or if you want to make a before and after comparison, you can restore the map as it was before you used the undo function.

 

Edit Map Settings ...

After clicking on this menu item, a new window will appear where you can choose whether you want to create a single player map (Scenario) or a multiplayer map (Free Map). In addition, you'll be able to choose from a list box in this window how many Settlers there are at the begininning of the game and how many goods are initially available to them.

The Edit Map Settings function can only be used as long as you have not placed any buildings on the map during the editing process. As soon as you have manually placed a building on the map, for example, a guard tower, you have to manually edit the rest: soldiers, workers, special occupations and piles of goods. If you don't place any buildings on the map manually, each of the players begins with a guard tower and the default amount of goods and Settlers that you have chosen in the "Edit Map Settings" window.

Remember that as soon as you have manually placed a building for one player, this has to be done for the other players as well, because the program will disable the default functions for the amount of goods and Settlers for all players as soon as you have placed a building on the map.

Strategy Corner
A lot of effort is involved in defining every last detail for several different players, but the efforts you put into this fine tuning can do wonders for your map. As you become more experienced, you'll discover how you can send players into the field with very different starting conditions and still have a balanced game.

In order to minimize errors, there is a menu point "Set Basic Piles" in the Tool Window. Using this feature, you can place a group of goods piles on your map where you can be sure that nothing important is missing. You can naturally still make changes to these goods piles.

 

Edit Player Settings...

 

General

Editing player settings involves creating players, assigning these players to teams and giving them their starting positions.

In the title bar of the dialogue box you'll find a combination of letters and numbers following the title "Player Settings". This combination has the form P(x/y) S(x/y) with "x" and "y" standing for numbers. The first part, the P(x/y), tells you that player "x" of altogether "y" players is active. The second part, the S(x/y) tells you that you are working on the "x" constellation of altogether "y" constellations. Constellations will be described in more detail further below in the section "Multi Team Constellations".

When you create a new map, there will only be one player represented on the map with a red thumbtack. Creating a map for one player is possible if the map is intended for a perfectly peaceful colonization, free of  stress and free of any confrontation. But, apart from its value as a training mission, a one-player map will rarely offer the same excitement and challenges that a multiplayer map does.

Once you have set the size of the map, it can't be changed afterwards. All the Player Settings except for race, however, can be changed at any time. You can no longer change the race for a player after you've assigned this player buildings or settlers.

 

Preparation

Before working in the design area it is advisable to make a sketch of the map you want to create. This sketch should include the outlines of the islands (if you have more than one), the mountains and rocky terrain and the mineral resources. Further detail at this point is not necessary, but the placement of forests, deserts, stone fields could be helpful for you in visualizing possibilities for strategic planning of these elements.

After you've done this, you should carefully consider where you want to establish the starting positions of the different players. This is also the right time to give thought to possible alliances between the players. Keep in mind that players can be either human or computer controlled and all the various possibilities of forming alliances are open to you.

 

Setting the Start Positions for your Players

Whether you begin designing your map by first drawing the landmasses and then locating your players on these or by locating your players first and then drawing the landmasses around them is essentially a matter of personal preference. Drawing the landmasses first, however, will probably let you orient yourself more easily.

Assuming that you have begun with the rough draft of your landmasses, drawing in the grass and desert and possibly raising your first mountains, you are ready to establish your players' starting positions. To do this, click on the Edit menu and then Edit Player Settings (or use the keyboard shortcut Ctrl P). A new window will appear where you'll see the default settings for the first player, with race set for Romans, and Start X and Start Y set for default values corresponding to the middle of the size of the map you have chosen. Assuming that you have chosen a map size of 256 x 256, the default values for the first player will be Start X: 128 and Start Y: 128.

Here you can either accept the default values for the first player playing the Romans, beginning in the middle of the map or you can enter new values. In the list box in the Player Data area to the right of the item Race, you will find a choice of Romans, Egyptians, Asians, Amazons or Choose. When you select "Choose", the race will not be assigned, but rather left open for the (human) player to choose when he/she enters the game. Below this list box, you'll see two boxes where you can enter the starting position for the first player. Start X sets the location on the horizontal axis of the map and Start Y on the vertical axis. When entering the values for the players' starting positions, you'll notice that if you enter values that correspond to a position outside the map, the program will automatically reassign the values to the closest position within the range of acceptable values, that is, within the map area.

There is also an alternative method for setting the start positions of the players. Assuming that you have two larger islands, one in the north and one in the south, and that you have set the magnification of the map at "x4" for a more detailed view of say the southern island, you can click on the button "Use current screen position" and the program will automatically enter the Start X / Y values based on the center of the window currently displayed in the work area.

If this position is not exactly the one you want, you can naturally reenter other values in the boxes for Start X and Start Y manually. The higher the X value you enter, the further right the start position will be; The higher the Y value you enter, the lower the start position will be.

You can set a start position in water. If you do this, the map won't be functional unless you draw land below this point afterwards. Then it will function as a legitimate start position.

The following guidelines should be observed when you set the starting points. If any of these points are not observed, the Level Editor may refuse to export your map.

If the player already has a building:

  • The terrain for the starting position must be grass. For the Egyptians, it may also be desert.
  • The terrain on which the starting position lies must be free of buildings and objects.
  • The terrain on which the starting position lies must belong to the corresponding player.
    •

    If the player doesn't yet have a building:

  • approx. a 70 field radius around the starting point must be grass.
  • approx. a 70 field radius around the starting point may not belong to another player.
  • approx. a 70 field radius around the starting point may not contain objects or buildings.
    •

    If the player doesn't yet have a building, it is important that the starting point doesn't lie in an area with an extreme inclines because the starting tower cannot be placed there.

    The start position of your first player is now ready, but before continuing with the settings for further players, a couple of other values can be set.

     

    Setting up Teams

    Here again you'll want to give some thought to how you want to organize team constellations. You can create as many teams as you have players if you want a free-for-all with all the players pitted against each other. If you do not create any teams, all players will be allies and no attacks or battles will be possible. Apart from the free-for-all variant, you can create a game in which several players play as a team, allied with one another. When you assign two or more players the same number in the Team box, these players play on the same team.

    In the default settings, the team number is set at zero for all players. If you do not change the values here, all players will be allied and there will not be any opponents.

    You can also decide here whether and in which constellation teams are formed with humans and computers, or leaving the decision free for whoever might use your map for a multiplayer game. You'll find the options, human, computer and choose in the "Played by" box.

     

    Player Names

    If the player chosen is a human, as opposed to a computer opponent, the space below "Computer Player Name" can be left free.

    Later when a player enters the game, he/she can use his/her own name. If, however, this player is to be played by the computer, a name must be entered so that the computer player can be identified. What you decide to name the computer player, Johnny, R2-D2 or whatever is up to you. The name cannot be more than 32 characters long, though.

    If you want to create further players, click on the "Add Player" button. You'll notice that the number in the Player box has increased by 1. Click on the "OK" button to return to the Design Area and from there you can center the map on the start position you want for the next player. Then the process can be repeated as before for all further players. Be sure that the correct player number is entered in the "Tool Setup Window" in the box to the right of "Selected Player".

     

    Multi Team Constellations

     

    General

    It is possible that you might want to place different team configurations on the map you are designing. If, for example, you are creating a map for six players, three human and three computer, you can group the humans against the computers in one constellation and group each human with a computer in another constellation. So that you don't have to save each map separately under different names, the Map Editor allows you to configure different team constellations and save each of these in a single file. The different constellations of your map will then be displayed in the game with the names you have given them

    You can only make use of different team constellations for multiplayer maps. If you are currently designing a single player map, the relevant buttons will be deactivated.

     

    Creating Different Team Constellations

    Different team constellations consist only of differing numbers of teams and numbers of players on each team.

    You cannot save different constellations with different start positions or different numbers of players. It is possible, however, to deactivate single players. In this case these players are theoretically still present, but no longer appear in the game.

    You can deactivate a player, by clicking on the "Disable" button in the "Player Settings" Window. A checkmark will appear in the box indicating that the relevant player in this constellation is not active.

    An example:

    You have saved the first constellation with the following values:

    Player Number Player Team Number
    Player 1 Human Team 0
    Player 2 Computer Team 0
    Player 3 Human Team 1
    Player 4 Computer Team 1
    Player 5 Human Team 2
    Player 6 Computer Team 2
    Player 7 Human Team 3
    Player 8 Computer Team 3
    Player 9 Human Team 4
    Player 10 Computer Team 4

    You now want to save another constellation of the same map. First you must give this map a constellation name. This name should include a short description of the distribution of the teams. A name such as "5 x 2players(H/C)" would indicate that there are five teams, each made up of 1 human (H) and 1 (C) computer player.

    In order to create a second constellation, click on the "Add Setup" button. You will now notice that there is a 2 in the Setup display box. Assume now that you want to create a constellation of three teams, each team consisting of two humans and one computer player. Below you can see the changes made to achieve these results.

    Player Number Player Team Number
    Player 1 Human Team 0
    Player 2 Computer Team 0
    Player 3 Human Team 0
    Player 4 Computer Team 1
    Player 5 Human Team 1
    Player 6 Human Team 1
    Player 7 Human Team 2
    Player 8 Computer Team 2
    Player 9 Human Team 2
    Player 10 disabled    

    To make these changes you only have to choose the players that you want to assign to a new team and then change the number in the Team box for the appropriate team. You can, of course, disable any of the players you want to obtain the desired results.

    You always have to begin team numbering with 0 and you cannot skip any numbers. Making four teams with the numbers 1, 3, 4, 5 is not possible. The Map Editor will not accept this numbering when you try to save this team constellation.

    After you have completed your second constellation of teams, don' t forget to give it an appropriate name such as 3x3 Players (2H/1C). Altogether you can create up to ten different team constellations using the same map.

    The changes you make only apply to the active player and the active constellation. Before you create further constellations, you should be sure that you don't want to carry out any further changes in the existing one. Click through all the players and check their settings again.

     

    Edit Victory Conditions ...

     

    General

    Victory conditions can only be edited when you create a single player map. In multiplayer games the victory always goes to the player or team that defeats all of the others. For single player games you can devise a variety of victory conditions.

    You can combine victory conditions. You can, for example, set the conditions that a player must gain control of a certain number of locations AND has to get a certain amount of goods into storage. You only have to enable the respective boxes under "Locations" and "Goods".

    After you click on the item, Edit Victory Conditions, a window will appear containing five tabs: Opponents, Buildings, Locations, Time, Goods and Description.

     

    Opponents

    This dialog window contains a tic box "Enable Victory Condition: Annihilate Players" which you can activate by checking. Below is a listing of players numbered from 1 to 20. Check the boxes of those players who must be defeated to attain victory. Be sure that you don't check any of your allies because you won't be able to attack them and therefore you won't be able to defeat them either.

     

    Buildings

    This dialog window also contains a tic box "Enable Victory Condition: Destroy Buildings" which you can activate by selecting. In the lists below you can enter the Player number of your opponents and immediately to the right what kind of buildings that have to be fully destroyed, for example, all harbor buildings. You can set up to 10 conditions for a like number of opponents.

     

    Locations

    This dialog window also contains a tic box "Enable Victory Condition: Control Specific Locations" which you can activate by selecting. Here you can set up to five locations which must be occupied in order to win. These locations are defined as x / y coordinates on your map. Each of the locations must be checked "on" to become activated.

     

    Time

    The tic box in this dialog window is called "Enable Victory Condition: Time", which you activate by selecting. Below is a listing of 20 Players, for whom you can assign survival times. When, for example, the first player is controlled by a human and is allied with a second player, a victory condition could be entered that the second player has to survive for 120 minutes. It is in the interests of the first player to help keep the second player alive during this time period or the game is lost for this team.

    During the game, the time which you have survived will be displayed on a stop watch which you'll find in the lower right-hand corner of your screen.

     

    Goods

    In this dialog window you can define the victory condition so that the human player must amass a certain amount of goods in order to win. You can choose up to three different goods to vary this victory condition.

    You don't necessarily have to produce these goods. You can also obtain them through conquest or theft. It is, therefore, possible to set a victory condition in which a good is chosen that the selected race can't even produce, for example, Asians amassing beer.

     

    Description

    Here you'll find a text window where you can describe the victory conditions for your map. This description will then appear in the game so that others playing your map know what they have to do to win.

     

    Set All Resources to Default

    By clicking on this menu item you can distribute raw materials throughout your map on a random basis. Fish will only be distributed in waters; gold, iron, coal, sulfur and gems in rocky terrain.

    NOTE ON STRATEGY:
    This function can save you a lot of time, but at the same time you lose the opportunity of using the distribution of raw materials as a strategic element.

     

    Remove All Resources

    If, on closer inspection, you decide that you aren't satisfied with the distribution of resources and that you'd prefer to begin all over from the beginning with their distribution, you can remove all of your fish, coal, gold, gems, iron and sulfur from the map by clicking on this menu item.

     

    Roughen Landscape...

    If you have not worked on the characteristics of the terrain on your map, it will be flatter than the West Texas Plains. That can look very unappealing, especially considering how attractive THE SETTLERS III world is. To give your landscape a little character, you can have the computer vary the level of the terrain. Like all tools that work on a random basis, however, the result is not always optimal.

    For getting started, a couple grouped settings for the options follow which can give you an initial feeling for working with this function, however. For the following options use either the group 1settings together or the group 2 settings. A mixture of the two can again lead to unpredictable results. Don't be afraid to experiment with this function to find your favorite settings.

    This function is only available as long as you haven't placed any buildings on the map. Take precaution that a particular building doesn't end up on terrain that is too uneven for it.

    It is also not advisable to use this function AFTER you have already raised your terrain to form mountains. In this case the mountains could be flattened as a result.

    After clicking on the "Roughen Landscape" item, a window will appear in which you can adjust the parameters which will affect how your landscape looks after you have automatically roughened it. You can adjust the following factors.

     

    Altitude Differences

    With this tool you can set the elevation changes between the highest and lowest points on the map.

    • Recommendation 1: Medium
    • Recommendation 2: Medium

     

    Options

    You can create mountain peaks with extreme inclines on a random basis using the mountain mode. These mountain chains are generally unsuitable for mining activities because even for mines, the inclines are too steep. These types of mountains are primarily used for creating interesting visuals, and not for practical reasons.

    The option "Brush variation" causes the program to change the terrain using all the brush sizes which are no larger than ones you have chosen under "Brush Size".

    • Recommendation 1: Brush variation
    • Recommendation 2: Brush variation

     

    Number of Peaks

    You can choose here whether you prefer a more varied terrain with many small hills (High) or a more uniform terrain with softer gradations (Low).

    • Recommendation 1: High
    • Recommendation 2: Low

     

    Brush Size

    Here you can adjust the width of the brush you use when locating materials on your map.

    • Recommendation 1: Small
    • Recommendation 2: Bold

     

    Check Map

    Creating a SETTLERS III map involves a number of factors and one or the other factor can easily be overseen. As a checkup, a program routine has been built in to determine whether the map you have created is functional. When you click on this menu item, appropriate error reports and warnings will be displayed. The error reports show mistakes which have to be corrected so that the map is playable. Examples of errors on a map would be the presence of more than 255 islands (the game program can’t handle more than 255) or the assignment of two economic sectors to a computer player.

    In additon to checking the map, the Level Editor will provide the following information:

    Number of Separate Islands
    Separate islands are those landmasses which are surrounded either by water or snow. It isn't possible for a Settler to move between separate islands by foot.

    Number of Economic Sectors
    An economic sector is an non-separated area on one island. If we think of North America as an island in Settler terms, the United States would consist of two economic sectors, Alaska on the one hand and the lower 48 states on the other hand, because the two parts are separated by Canada. Each island and each separated territory on an island is considered an economic sector.

    Number of All Settlers (of each type and for every defined player)

    Number of All Goods Piles (of each type and for every defined player)

    Every economic sector which is surrounded by unpassable terrain counts as an island. An island then could be a small patch of rocky terrain surrounded by snow. These islands could be so small that they are scarcely noticeable in the Editor, especially in the more distant zoom views. For this reason it is advisable not to spray snow, but rather to disable the spray function when you place snow on your map. Otherwise, more than 255 islands could be unintentionally created.

    Warnings indicate POSSIBLE problems which are not necessarily real errors. The program gives a warning, for example, when a player begins with fewer than two smiths because the hammers lying on the ground will be taken immediately by builders and then there will be no hammers for the smiths. You can design a map with only one smith, (a tool smith!), who can then produce further tools or even a map with no smith at all.

    You can find more information about this in the section "Error Reports and Warnings".

     

    The "View" Menu

    The "View" menu contains twelve items: Show Tool Window, Show Setup Window, Show Objects, Show Buildings, Show Settlers, Show Goods Piles, Normal Object Size, Show Building Outlines, Show Start Positions, Piles With Numbers, Resource View and Island View.

    Show Tool Window and Show Tool Setup Window

    The two menu items in this group allow you to either show or hide the Tool Window and the Tool Setup Window. Whether you want to select a certain terrain, change its elevation, place trees, settlers, buildings, goods or resources on your map, you find it here; almost everything you could possibly want to place on your map can be found in the Tool Window. You'll find a complete listing of all the elements in this window along with a description of their characteristics in the section "A Closer Look at the Tool Setup Window".

    In the Tool Setup Window you can adjust the width of your brush, whether you want to paint using a solid brush or spray, and in the special case of buildings and settlers, you can choose for which player you are placing them.

    You cannot place buildings or non-military settlers outside their own territory. The program will refuse to do this. If you find that you cannot place a guard tower on the map although there is adequate space, check whether the color of the player in the Tool Setup Window corresponds to the color of the territory where you are currently trying to place it. Soldiers, geologists, thieves and pioneers can be placed outside their territories as well as inside them.

     

    Show Objects, Buildings, Settlers, Goods piles

    The four menu items in this group allow you to either show or hide objects such as stones, trees and plants, the various buildings, your settlers and piles of goods in the Design Area. Each of these four can be independently activated or deactivated according to your needs. These functions can also be more quickly activated from the icon bar. They are, respectively from left to right the four icons with a tree, a building, a settler soldier, and three crates.

    The "Show Settlers" function shows or hides Settlers, cannons, ballistas, catapults, gongs and ships.

    Sometimes you may wish to hide some of the objects you've placed on your map so that you can find or identify others. Buildings can conceal settlers, and pioneers can easily "get lost" in a thick forest. You can either click through the menu "View" or you can toggle the appropriate buttons on the icon bar.

     

    Normal Object Size

    This menu item allows you to magnify the size of all objects, settlers, buildings, piles of goods, and free resources. This function allows you to more easily identify everything that you've placed on your map.

     

    Show Building Outlines

    This menu item allows you to see the actual space that a building requires to be placed somewhere on the map. In most cases, except where space is really at a premium, you'll be able place your buildings without having to use this function.

     

    Show Start Positions

    This menu item allows you to view or hide the start positions of the various players as represented in the Design Area with a colored thumbtack.

     

    Piles with numbers

    This menu item allows you to view or hide numbers on the goods piles indicating how many pieces of each good are in the pile.You can, of course, hide or view the goods piles themselves with the menu item "Show Goods Piles".

     

    Resource View

    This menu item allows you to focus on the resources you've placed on your map, hiding all other objects.

     

    Island View

    When you click on this item, all "islands" (isolated economic sectors) on your map will be displayed in red. This will help you to identify quickly where passages may be blocked or whether you have too many "islands" on your map. The Level Editor allows a maximum of 255 "islands".

    All of the areas which aren't shown in red are blocked. This means that your Settlers including soldiers, thieves, geologists and pioneers have no access to these areas.

     

    The "Help" Menu

    The "Help" menu contains two items, Online Documentation and About THE SETTLERS III Level Editor.

     

    SIEDLER III Leveleditor Hilfe

    This menu item allows you to access this online manual from THE SETTLERS III Level Editor.

     

    Info

    This menu item gives you details regarding copyright information and also the name of the person who wrote this program.
