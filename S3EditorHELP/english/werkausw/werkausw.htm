A Closer Look at the Tool Setup Window

The Tool Window allows you to terraform your Settlers world in a variety of ways. This includes changing the elevation of the terrain, placing a wide variety of trees, plants, raw materials, finished goods, buildings and, of course, your initial population of Settlers onto the map in the Design Area. You can adjust the diameter of the brushes and choose whether to use a solid or spray application. In the special case of setting buildings and settlers, you also have to setup the player for the buildings or settlers you are placing on the map. These two categories, buildings and settlers, are specific to each player. Terrain, other objects, and raw materials are general for the entire map.

 

Tool Setup

Brushes

Four different sizes of brushes are available: point: 1 x 1, thin: 3 x 3, medium: 6 x 6, and bold: 12 x 12.

For greater precision you also can check the option to the right, "Customized". Then you can choose freely any dimension ranging from 1 x 1 to 25 x 25 in the option box directly below.

Below the option box for Customized, there is a check box designated "Sprinkle". By checking this box, you can paint transitions between different types of terrain so that they appear more natural. The brush size is a bit narrower and more opaque using this option.

The brush sizes are deactivated when you are placing buildings, ships and war machines on the map because these are always placed individually. When you place these objects or delete them, your settings remain unchanged.

 

Spray

You can also give your brushes an airbrush effect. The diameter of your brush is then quadrupled. In the box, Spray Density, you can enter a value between 1 and 25 which determines the how thick a spray is applied.

The following list contains the objects or functions, which are not suitable for the spray function. The spray function either leads to absurd results or can make designing a good map more difficult. The Level Editor will warn you before you use the spray function in combination with the following items.

  • Ships
  • Instruments of War
  • Height Tools
  • Delete Buildings
  • Set Buildings
  • Edit Foundation Buildings
  • Set Basic Piles
  • Set Settler

    Player

    By clicking on the "Edit Players" button, you can edit the characteristics of the buildings and settlers that you are assigning for the current player. After you have defined several players, you can switch back and forth between the different players from this button in the "Tool Setup" Window. This button opens the menu, "Player Settings".

    You can neither set the current player's civilian settlers or buildings in foreign territory. The Level Editor will not allow this. If you have difficulties placing a guard tower on the map although there is more than enough space, check whether the color of the chosen player and the color of the territory match.

    Soldiers, geologists, thieves and pioneers can naturally be placed within or outside their home territories.

     

    Set Type of Terrain

    Altogether, seven types of terrain are available to you for creating your SETTLERS III world. This includes grassland, desert, scree (rocky terrain at the base of mountains), swampland, bogs (muddy terrain), snowy terrain and beaches (Shore). Rivers and Water (Seas) are also included in this group.

    In the Tool Window there is an item "Delete All". When this item is selected, you'll delete all objects and settlers when you glide over them with your mouse in the Design Area. This item doesn't delete raw materials, buildings, the terrain or height tool settings.

     

    Grass

    Objects for this kind of terrain: Everything except cactus plants, skeletons, shipwrecks and reeds.

    After you have loaded a new map and selected its size, you can begin by choosing from the "Tool Window" under "Set Type of Terrain" the item, grass. In the "Tool Setup Window", choose the brush size thin, making sure that neither the option "speckle" nor "spray" is activated. You are now ready to paint the contours of your map in the Design Area. This should appear in the form of a green line with yellow borders. The green areas will later be displayed in the game as grass and the yellow areas as shoreline.

    Now change the brush size to medium or bold and fill in the area until your island is a large, flat grassland.

    Grass is the basis for all your worlds. You can place almost all objects on grass and is important in allowing your settlement to expand.

    FOR BETTER-LOOKING MAPS: Before continuing it is advisable to roughen up the coastline by selecting a thin brush and activating the speckle option in your Tool Setup Window. When you look at your island in the game, you may notice that when you do not roughen up your coasts, they will tend to look as if they've been drawn with a ruler and consequently, will look pretty boring and unnatural.

    Click on the menu item "View Map in Game" in the "File" menu to see how the coast looks in game view.

     

    Desert

    Objects for this kind of terrain: no plants except for cactus plants, flowers, small bushes and palm trees, and skeletons. The Egyptians are the only race who can build on desert land.

    Deserts, as well as swamps and muddy terrain should be well planned. Deserts offer the Egyptians great advantages and, in contrast, can present other races with insurmountable barriers since they can not build in the desert. The strategic role that deserts play should not be underestimated.

     

    Rocky Terrain

    Objects for this kind of terrain: none.

    If you've already raised some mountains with the "Height Tools" from your Tool Window, you can define the areas where nothing grows and only mines can be built. No objects are allowed on this type of terrain, but it is nevertheless of critical importance due to what’s found below the surface. Mineral resources, such as iron, coal, gold, sulfur, and also gems are only located in rocky terrain. You only have to convert the surface into rocky terrain.

     

    Swamp

    Objects for this type of terrain: none except for reeds.

    None of the races can build in swamps. For the Asian, nevertheless, they do play an important role in rice farming. Similar to desert for the Egyptians, swampland offers the Asians advantages but on the other hand hinders movement and transport for all the races. For this reason swamps have a strategic role comparable to deserts.

     

    Mud

    Objects for this kind of terrain: none.

    Mud can, in contrast to swamp, be built upon. Mud slows down your settlers considerably, though. In general, mud is simply a big nuisance for your settlers. For the design of the map, though, it offers interesting strategic potential.

     

    Snow

    Objects for this kind of terrain: none.

    Snow can only be placed on rocky terrain, and also cannot be crossed by your settlers. What this means for your settlers on the one hand and what it means for you as a map designer thinking strategically should go without saying.

    Small "islands" of rocky terrain in the snow are viewed as separate islands by the Level Editor. If you apply snow using the speckle function, make sure that you don't unintentionally create a lot of small "islands". Your map will not be functional if it has more than 255 islands. We recommend disabling the "Sprinkle" and "Spray" functions or using them very sparingly.

     

    Rivers

    Objects for this kind of terrain: none.

    Rivers within reasonable distance are of critical importance to all the players your create. Without rivers there can be no food, no mines and therefore no weapons or tools. Without water there can also be no beer or rice wine.

    Rivers can be "painted" on the map in four widths. Although it is, of course, true that a river normally is wider from its source to its mouth, rivers on settler maps look best when they vary their width, sometimes narrower, sometimes widening into a small lake. A small delta at the mouth of the river also adds a realistic touch.

    Rivers, despite being displayed in various widths in the game, are not displayed in the Level Editor as more than one pixel wide. The width of the river can only be set from the "Tool Window".

     

    Shore

    Objects for this kind of terrain: shipwrecks and palm trees.

    A beach can offer a delightful view and it can also contribute to the realism of your map as well. Again, be careful that your coasts don't end up looking like they have been drawn with a ruler. Remember that no buildings can be constructed on shore.

    Don't create too many sandbars in the water. The Level Editor regards these as separate islands. In addition, goods or soldiers could be unloaded from ships onto the sandbars and possibly stranded.

     

    Water

    Objects for this kind of terrain: reefs.

    The Level Editor offers you eight different depths of water so that your seas can look as realistic as your land. This is primarily a matter of appearance, but that is certainly not unimportant.

     

    Height Tools

    The planning for the placement of mountains on your map should take place at an early stage. Keep in mind that mountains present considerable obstacles for your settlers. Guard towers cannot be built there and this makes expanding territory more difficult. The strategic aspects of mountains shouldn't be underestimated.

    In addition to the Roughen Landscape tool, you now have six additional tools at your disposal for adjusting the elevation of the landmasses on your map. These are:

  • Raise or Lower Terrain (Hard)
  • Raise or Lower Terrain (Medium)
  • Raise or Lower Terrain (Soft)
    •

    The hard setting raises or lowers your landmasses with sharp degrees of slope, which can make the construction of buildings, including mines, impossible. This tool should only be used sparingly because a mineral resources located under terrain where you cannot build are worthless.

    You should, therefore, use the soft and medium settings for raising and lowering the terrain on your map. Your mountains may take up a little bit more space but they look better and offer more possibilities for locating mineral resources that can be mined.

     

    Set Buildings

    General

    After you have created the different types of terrain and have adjusted their elevation, it is time to begin defining the players that will be playing on the map, unless you want to begin play with the default values for goods and Settlers that the Level Editor offers.

    As soon as you have placed a building for one player on the map, you have to do this for all players. The Level Editor can set default values for all parties but cannot set default values for only individual players.

    You can only place buildings in a settlement when the correct player is activated in the Tool Setup window. If you want to place a building in the settlement of player 1, player 1 has to be activated.

    After you have clicked on a building in the "Tool Window" under "Set Buildings", you'll see that a symbol of this building is attached to your mouse pointer when you move it over your map in the Design Area. When this building symbol is in color, you can place the building where your mouse pointer is currently located. If the building symbol is represented in gray tones, the Level Editor will not allow you to place the building where your mouse pointer currently is.

     

    Reasons why a building cannot be set

    When you are not able to place a building on your map, check the following list of points to determine the reason.

  • The construction site for the building is located outside the settlement.
  • The wrong party is activated.
  • The construction site for the building is located on unsuitable terrain.
  • The construction site for the building is too small
  • The construction site for the building is too steep.
  • The construction site for the building is already occupied by another object or settler.
  • The party chosen has not yet been assigned to a race.

    Foundation Buildings

    Setting Foundation Buildings

    Foundation buildings include the large and small guard towers as well as the castles. Foundation buildings are those which allow you to expand your settlement. These are the only buildings that you can place outside the territory of the player that is currently activated. This doesn't apply to the territory occupied by another player, however.

    If you delete a foundation building, it will result in a loss of territory for the player involved. This, in turn, will lead to all other buildings and settlers in the area of this foundation building also being deleted. The undo function is not available when you delete a foundation building.

    Players played by the computer are not allowed to have two or more separate colonies! The program would not interpret them as separate and would not be able to exchange goods between the two. The result would be a computer opponent with scarcely any chances of survival.

    If you choose to place a tower or castle for a particular player, this player must be activated in the "Tool Setup Window"! A foundation building always defines a specific area of control for the currently active player.

     

    Editing Foundation Buildings

    You can edit the number and type of soldiers in your foundation buildings. To do this, click on the menu item, "Edit Garrison", under "Foundation Buildings" and then click on the building in the Design Area that you want to edit, provided, of course, that you have already placed a building on your map. In the menu that appears, you can set exactly how many soldiers of which type and of which rank are to occupy this building from the beginning of the game. Please keep in mind that each building must be occupied by at least one swordsman, and that you cannot garrison more soldiers in a building than it is intended to hold.

     

    Service Buildings

    The buildings of the Service industry include the Woodcutter's hut, Stonecutter's hut, Saw mill, Forester's hut, Wine farm (R), Grain mill, Bakery, Slaughterhouse, Distillery (A), Pig farm, Grain farm, Fisherman's hut, Waterworks, Rice farm (A), Brewery (E), and Donkey Ranch, Beekeeper's hut (Z) and Mead maker's works(Z).

     

    Mines & Tools

    The buildings of the Mining and Tool industry include the Coal mine, Gold mine, Ore mine, Gem mine (E / Z), Sulfur mine (A / Z), Gold smelting works, Iron smelting works, Tool maker's works, Weapon maker's works, Gun powder maker's works (A), and the Coal maker's works. (R)

    Mines can only be placed on rocky terrain. Make sure that if you place mines on your map that they are located above deposits of the appropriate mineral. It might be a good idea to place your mines and their deposits at the same time so that later a player does not wonder why the gold mine, for instance, which was present from the beginning of the game has not produced one basket of gold ore.

    The Mining and Tool industry is meant to include the mines, smelting works, as well as the tool and weapon smith's works. It doesn't include the halls for the instruments of war, which can be found in the following menu.

     

    Military and Residential Buildings

    The buildings in this group include Small residences, Medium residences, Large residences, the Catapult Hall (R), the Ballista Hall (E), the Cannon Hall (A), the Gong Hall (Z), Barracks, and Lookout tower.

    You don't have to create residential buildings for the settlers that you place on your map for the beginning of the game. If you place a residential building on your map, new settlers will storm out at the very beginning of the game.

     

    Special Buildings

    The special buildings include the Storage Area, Shipyard, Harbor, Marketplace, Healer's Quarters, the Alchemist's Den (Z), Pyramid (E), Sphinx (E), Big Temple (R / Z), Small Temple (R / Z), Big Pagoda (A), and the Small Pagoda (A).

     

    Points to remember when you place buildings on your map

    Remember that computer players are not allowed to have two separate islands or colonies.

    Give your settlers enough room to settle. When two players bump into each other early in the game, the game is seldom as much fun as it could be.

    Computer players should normally be given somewhat of a "head start", as they are clearly slower than their human counterparts at building up a colony. Give them a couple mines, a tool smith's works and a weapon smith's works that the human players have to build for themselves. You'll find additional information in the section "The Computer Opponent"

    Remember to place the appropriate handworker on the map for each building you place on your map.

     

    Setting Objects

    After you have placed buildings on your map, landscape objects are next on the agenda. It is a good idea to differentiate between the practical ones, mainly trees and stone which you need as building materials, and the more decorative ones, bushes and reeds, for example.

    Not all kinds of stone can be used as building material. The stones that are suitable for construction are listed under Building Stone. All other types of stone serve to enhance the settings, or can be used strategically as obstacles.

    Don't forget that graves, shipwrecks and toadstools, although not of any value for building purposes, can not be dismissed as purely decorative additions to the landscape. Without these, your priests won't be able to perform some acts of divine intervention that could change the course of a game.

    Border stones are valuable in that they can block entire passages because they fit so tightly together that no one can get by them.

    Auto Generation
    An area where you only see the same kinds of stone or trees is pretty boring. Placing each tree or stone on the map individually is fairly tedious. The Auto Generation function deals with these two problems by producing either a variety of objects which are appropriate for a forest, a stone field or a desert or also a variety of building stone which can be placed either on grassy terrain or desert.

    Auto Generation is particularly effective with the "Spray" function activated.

     

    Setting Settlers

    General

    You can only place settlers within the borders of their own colonies. If the Level Editor doesn't allow you to place a settler in a particular location, that is probably because you have the wrong party activated or the party you have chosen hasn't been assigned to a definite race. The settlers connected with the military, that is, soldiers, thieves, pioneers and geologists are exceptions to the rule. These settlers can be placed outside the borders of their own colonies and for this reason, you should be particularly careful that you don't inadvertently position a horde of enemy soldiers in one player's territory.

    You can set the correct player in the "Tool Setup Window".

    The function of predefined workers is designed to prevent your settlement from running into a dead-end early in the game.

    An example: You have placed a group of builders and a couple of hammers at the starting point for your colony. As soon as the game begins, the builders begin to construct buildings. You don't know how many buildings and how many builders are needed before you build a toolmaker's works. In all probability a residence will be already be finished. One or more of the new settlers streaming out grabs a hammer and becomes a builder. Then you build your toolmaker's works and realize that no toolmaker reports for duty. Unfortunately, there are no longer any hammers available. Still worse, there is no toolmaker that could produce a hammer. Your game has run into a dead end.

    A table follows which offers some helpful orientation for ensuring that your map doesn't lead to dead ends on account of settler problems.

    Miners 2 4 6

    For all other Settler occupations it is normally enough to make the appropriate tool available. More information about this follows in the next section.

     

    Setting Resources

    The location of raw materials plays a very important strategic role and will vary considerably from map to map. It is most important to keep in mind that the distribution of resources among the different parties should be made so that none of the parties in the game has a disadvantage that is at least not balanced by another factor as an advantage.

    You place raw materials on your map in the same way that you have placed other objects on your map. Choose the resource from the "Tool Window" and click on the desired location on your map. Remember that certain resources can only be placed in certain areas. Fish can only be placed in seas and mineral resources can only be placed in rocky terrain.

     

    Please note that you choose which resources you want to place on your map from the menu item, Resource Piles, and the quantity of the resources from the menu item, "Set Pile Quantity". The default value for each deposit is set at 8 units. This amount can be adjusted from 1 to 15 in the menu item "Set Pile Quantity" in the "Tool Window" by selecting either of the items, Increase Amount or Decrease Amount. A small light bar on the right-hand border of the resource symbol will show you the currently set value. Increasing the zoom level will make it easier for you to see how many resources you have actually buried so far.

    The symbols for some resources have different colors at different zoom levels for visual reasons. The following list provides orientation.

     

    Resource Zoomlevel x1 Zoomlevel x2 to x4
    Coal Black Black
    Gold Yellow Yellow
    Sulfur Ochre Ochre
    Gemse Red Red

    Setting Goods Piles

    General

    At the beginning of the game your settlers should have a pile of goods at their disposal to get them off on the right foot. Some resources are absolutely essential for getting a settlement off the ground, other goods can considerably ease the work of establishing a colony for a player so that an occasional oversight doesn't lead to an early dead end.

    In order to place goods on your map, choose the type of good from the "Tool Window" (under "Set Goods Piles") and then click on the location on your map where you want them. You can place goods wherever you want because they are not associated with a specific race or tribe. This offers you the opportunity to place a couple goodies in an otherwise unfavorable site as a reward for those who make the effort of settling this area.

    The goods piles that you place on your map are made up of eight units. It is possible to adjust this quantity in the menu item "Set Pile Quantity" by choosing the number that you want and then clicking on the pile that you want to change.

    You normally don't need the value "0" because you can delete piles of goods. If, however, you place a storage area on your map, goods piles will automatically be set there as well. These cannot be deleted. So that these goods don't appear in the game, you can set the value to "0".

    Setting Ships and Instruments of War

    Ships can only be placed in seas and only where the water depth is set to 6 or more. In addition, ships require a certain amount of water space around them.

    Ships are viewed as military units, which means that they can be placed everywhere and are not associated with a particular race or tribe. Here again, you have to be careful that when placing a ship on your map that you have the player activated in your "Tool Setup" Window for whom it is intended.

    The same thing applies for instruments of war. These can also be placed wherever there is enough space. Keep in mind that each race has its own specific instrument of war: gongs for the Amazons, catapults for the Romans, ballistas for the Egyptians and cannons for the Asians. The Level Editor won't allow you to place a cannon on your map, for example, when an Egyptian or Roman race is activated in the "Tool Setup" Window.
